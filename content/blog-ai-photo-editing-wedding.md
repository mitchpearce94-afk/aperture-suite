## Why Wedding Photography Is Hard for AI

Before getting into the tools, it's worth understanding why wedding photography specifically is tricky for AI editing.

**The style problem.** Your clients booked you because of how your photos look. Your editing style — the warmth in the shadows, the way you pull back on highlights, the skin tones you've spent years dialling in — that's not generic. Every AI editing tool has to learn your look, not just apply a one-size-fits-all treatment. The tools that do this well require training data. The question is how much.

**The volume problem.** A typical wedding produces 500-1,500 selects after culling. You need a tool that handles that volume reliably, consistently, and without requiring your laptop to run for eight hours.

**The variety problem.** A wedding shoots across multiple lighting conditions in a single day — bright outdoor ceremony, dark reception hall, golden hour portraits, flash-heavy first dance. Your AI editing has to handle all of it coherently and maintain your look throughout.

Not all tools handle all three of these well.

## The Tools: What They Are and What They're Missing

### Aftershoot

**What it is:** AI culling + editing, runs locally on your machine  
**Price:** ~$16/mo AUD  
**Strengths:** Good at culling (selecting your best images). Decent editing with style learning. Large community.  
**The problem:** It runs locally. On your computer. Which means if you shoot 3,000 images, your laptop is tied up processing for hours — sometimes overnight. For photographers on lower-spec machines, this is brutal. You can't use your computer while it's running, and the processing time negates a lot of the time savings.  
**Training requirement:** Around 500-1,000 edited images to get good style results.

**Best for:** Photographers with powerful desktop setups who primarily need culling and don't mind waiting.

---

### Imagen

**What it is:** Cloud-based AI editing (integrates with Lightroom)  
**Price:** $0.05/photo or subscription  
**Strengths:** Cloud-based (no laptop meltdown). Lightroom integration is smooth. Good accuracy once trained.  
**The problem:** The training requirement is punishing. Imagen recommends 3,000-5,000 edited image pairs to train your style profile properly. For photographers who are starting out, or who have multiple distinct styles for different clients, this is a massive barrier. You're looking at years of shooting before you have enough data.  
**Training requirement:** 3,000-5,000 images minimum for reliable results.

**Best for:** High-volume photographers with years of consistently-edited images who are willing to invest in training.

---

### Lightroom AI (built-in)

**What it is:** Adobe's built-in AI features — denoise, masking, subject selection  
**Price:** Included with Lightroom subscription  
**Strengths:** Excellent AI masking and subject detection. Denoise is genuinely impressive.  
**The problem:** It's not an editing workflow tool — it's a set of individual AI features. It doesn't learn your style or edit entire galleries automatically. You're still editing every photo.

**Best for:** Everyone — these features are worth using regardless of what else you're doing.

## What to Look For in an AI Editing Tool

Before you commit to anything, ask these questions:

**1. Where does it process?**  
Local = your laptop gets tied up. Cloud = it happens while you do something else. Cloud is almost always better for high volume.

**2. How many images do you need to train it?**  
If the answer is more than a few hundred, be realistic about whether you have those images and whether they're consistently edited enough to train from.

**3. Does it integrate with your other tools?**  
AI editing in isolation means you're still exporting to Lightroom, downloading, uploading to a gallery platform, and sending emails manually. The full time savings come when the editing is connected to the rest of your workflow.

**4. What happens to images the AI isn't confident about?**  
The good tools flag uncertain images for your review rather than guessing. Look for a review workflow that makes it easy to spot and correct these.
